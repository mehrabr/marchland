# MARCHLAND — Addendum W: The Last Scan
### Four critic rounds before the archive closes — traditional gamers, the minimal-rules emergentist, the historian's third pass, and everyone outside the tent

**Extends:** everything; closes the gap-scan the brief requested. Method: each round is a critic class, its strongest objections, and a disposition — **ADOPT** (new work, punchlisted), **MITIGATE** (an existing system answers it, but needs emphasis or exposure), or **ACCEPT-AND-STATE** (a real cost, owned on the box). Round 1 is grounded in fresh research: the *complaint corpus* of our nearest neighbors — we had mined what players love about Command Ops and Scourge of War; we had never mined what they hate.

---

## Round 1 — Users raised on traditional systems (the complaint corpus)

**1a. "The friendly AI is stupid, so I'm forced to micromanage."** The most damning recurring review in the C2 genre — Command: Modern Operations players calling the friendly AI "absolutely stupid without micromanagement" behind an interface whose intuitiveness claims "cannot be further from the truth." For us this is not a polish item; it is existential. A game whose covenant *forbids* piloting lives or dies on whether subordinates act sensibly when trusted — every officer misjudgment the player can't override is charged against the design, not the officer. **ADOPT:** the officer-AI gets its own battery (does a flank commander refuse a suicidal order? exploit an open flank unprompted? misread an ambiguous order in period-plausible ways rather than pathologically?), with a stated quality bar: *the AI must be wrong the way captains were wrong, never the way pathfinding is wrong.* This becomes milestone M1's twin.

**1b. "I sent the order — did anything hear me?"** Scourge of War players literally petitioned for what we specced in Addendum H: the stance button staying blue until the courier arrives, then turning gold — UI feedback distinguishing *order given* from *order received*. Players of the ancestor game independently demanded the order-in-flight state. **ADOPT (hardened):** every order renders its full lifecycle — drafted → sealed → rider away → delivered → acknowledged → acting — as Table objects (the rider miniature *is* the progress bar). No order ever silently in limbo.

**1c. "I needed community videos and a fan PDF to learn what my own commands do."** SoW veterans describe struggling to master the interface and hunting forum tutorials for a stance-effects table the manual never provided. **ADOPT:** doctrine vocabulary is taught *diegetically* — every order term is explainable by your own staff ("Ask the major what 'demonstrate' means" produces a soldier's answer, with the drill-ground sandbox to try it), the in-game manual is a period officer's handbook, and no behavior ships that only a wiki can explain.

**1d. "A misclick destroyed an hour of planning."** The fat-finger catastrophe, named in the same reviews. Our dispatch grammar is naturally resistant (orders are composed documents, not twitch clicks), but **ADOPT:** review-before-seal on every dispatch, recall-while-the-rider-is-in-reach, and no destructive single-click anywhere.

**1e. "The AI cheats."** Strategic Command players convinced the enemy "spots everything" — and once players believe it, trust never returns. We are structurally innocent (the enemy commander runs on the same belief DB and Front-tier lookahead) but innocence unproven is guilt. **MITIGATE → expose:** the post-battle Archive can open *the enemy commander's belief table* — show the player exactly what their opponent knew and when. "The AI plays under the covenant" becomes a verifiable claim, maybe the genre's first.

**1f. Pause, saves, sessions, seeds (self-scan, never specified).** Pausing the *world* is fine in single-player — latency is in-world time, not wall-clock, so the covenant survives pause; suspend-anywhere is mandatory (a siege is sixty days; respect adult schedules); session shapes published (a dispatch turn: minutes; a battle: 20–60). And **ADOPT — the seed-locking policy:** a battle's seed locks the moment battle is consented; reloading replays identically. Save-scumming can't reroll fate, only decisions — which is the design's whole morality anyway. Ironman remains an option, not a virtue signal.

## Round 2 — The Olleus-class critique (the minimal-rules emergentist)

The strongest version, in the voice of the historum thread's skeptic: *"Your cue weights — 3.2 for corpses, 2.4 for routers, 2.6 for threat-behind — are hand-tuned psychological coefficients. You mocked 'behavior is a stat' and then shipped behavior as eight stats with a normalization. Who validated dividing the body count by six?"*

**Disposition: partially TRUE, and owned.** The defenses that hold: the weights are *universal* — parameters of one shared human appraisal function, never varied per people, so they pass the loaded-dice test that the original critique was actually about; and they were calibrated once (Isandlwana) then frozen and survived Agincourt, Hastings, and the assault battery unchanged — cross-scenario survival under freeze is the validation the rebuttal demanded. What doesn't hold: pretending the specific values are *discovered* rather than fitted. **ADOPT — the sensitivity harness:** a tool that perturbs every class A constant ±30% and re-runs the full battery, publishing which results are robust (the Agincourt outcome should survive almost anything), which are fragile, and therefore which constants are *load-bearing assumptions*. The honest answer to "those numbers are arbitrary" is a table showing exactly how much each one matters — and any battery pass that exists only at the fitted value gets demoted to tension. This ships with the CI gate (milestone M5) and its output goes in the public battery documentation.

## Round 3 — The historian's third pass (new angles only; Q and T stand)

**3a. The steppe-shaped hole.** The resolver is validated entirely on *formed-battle cultures* — shield walls, lines, squares, breaches. Addendum C flagged the crypto-Hanson risk (a "universal" engine that quietly privileges the Western set-piece because that's all it was tested on) and nothing since has closed it. Horse-archer warfare is not a unit type; it's a *way of war* — refusing contact as method, the feigned retreat at operational scale, victory by exhaustion of the enemy's logistics rather than his line. **ADOPT:** two battery entries elevated to named status before any universality claim ships — Sphacteria (light troops dismantling hoplites: tests whether harassment mechanics can beat formation without new rules) and Carrhae (tests the full stack: horse-archer rotation against the frontage cap, arrow resupply as a logistics object — the camel train is the battle's real protagonist — and a march-model desert approach feeding the battle state). If the frozen constants can't produce Carrhae, the engine is provincial and we say so.

**3b. The campaign community.** Lynn's correction stands against us too: the people around an army — women, families, sutlers, servants — were a substantial fraction of its population, and our model renders them as carrying capacity and pursuit victims. The command tent's view again. **ADOPT (schema, not physics):** the train becomes people with receipts — labor that cooks and forages and nurses (camp-tick quality has hands), morale anchors (home_pull has faces), mouths in the equation they already were. Ties to the parked sack-state and camp-life items; named here as a representation gap, not just a fidelity one.

**3c. Coerced labor.** Calones, pioneers, enslaved porters — ubiquitous historically, currently invisible inside the word "carriers." **ADOPT:** carrier entries take a status field with its own receipts (coerced labor deserts toward freedom, not home; is fed worse by institutional custom; its treatment is a class D fact with consequences). The abstraction was sanitizing; the schema stops it.

**3d. Ritual as a consent gate.** Battle consent in the engine is tactical confidence; historically it was also omens, sacrifices, oath-days, sacred calendars — the Spartans arrived after Marathon because of the Karneia. **ADOPT (cheap, pure class D):** consent conditions may include ritual states; diviners are officers with a role; a refused sacrifice is a legible, period-true reason an army won't fight today — and a commander who marches anyway spends legitimacy. Data files, no engine change.

## Round 4 — Gamers outside the tent

**4a. Accessibility — the archive's largest true blind spot: twenty-two documents and not one word.** **ADOPT, as a spec:** colorblind-safe army identity (banner *shapes* and field patterns carry faction identity redundantly with color — period-true, since liveries did exactly this); the truth-audio channel gets a deaf/HoH equivalent (a captioned sound-picture: direction, intensity, kind — "musketry, sustained, left, nearer"); photosensitivity review of the Dive transition; dyslexia-aware typography for a reading-heavy game; and the one where we're accidentally strong — the headless text core makes full screen-reader play *genuinely achievable*, a first for the genre, because underneath the Window this game already is text. Budgeted as a feature, not a compliance pass.

**4b. Platforms.** The Table is touch-native and the dispatch loop suits sessions; mobile/Switch is a real second act after PC — noted, not scheduled. **4c. Localization risk.** The chronicle generator must be translation-aware from day one (grammar templates per language, never English string surgery) or the writing-of-history feature becomes English-only; flagged into the engineering risks. **4d. The share loop.** Memoir export and Archive replays with the ensemble slider are the growth engine for an audience that discovers games through creators; the demo *is* a battle in study mode — the most shareable slice teaching the most central verb. **4e. The action-game tourist's first hour** is already the forensic opening (Q); the answer to "why can't I fight" is the Front Rank station existing, priced.

---

## Verdict and consolidated punchlist

The scan found three classes of result. **Real blind spots:** accessibility (nothing in 22 documents), the seed-locking policy, the order-lifecycle UI as player-demanded rather than designer-preferred, localization of generated prose. **Critiques partially conceded:** the cue weights are fitted folk-psychology — universal and frozen, but fitted — answered with the sensitivity harness rather than denial; the steppe hole is open until Carrhae passes. **Confirmations:** the complaint corpus of our ancestors independently demands what the design already specifies (order-state feedback, taught doctrine, trustworthy subordinates), which is the most reassuring market evidence yet — the genre's players are already asking for this game, one grievance at a time.

**Punchlist (final, consolidated):** officer-AI battery with the "wrong like captains, never like pathfinding" bar (M1-twin) · order-lifecycle states as Table objects · diegetic doctrine teaching + drill ground · review-before-seal and rider-recall · enemy-belief disclosure in the Archive ("the AI plays under the covenant") · pause/suspend/session spec · seed-locked battles, ironman optional · the sensitivity harness in CI with published load-bearing-assumption table · Sphacteria and Carrhae battery entries before universality claims · campaign-community schema (the train has faces) · coerced-labor status receipts · ritual consent gates and the diviner role · the accessibility spec (banner-shape identity, captioned sound-picture, Dive photosensitivity, screen-reader path) · translation-aware chronicle architecture · memoir/replay share formats and the study-mode demo.
