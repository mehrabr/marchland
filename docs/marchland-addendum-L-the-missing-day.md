# MARCHLAND — Addendum L: The Missing Day
### A gap audit against the historians — what the system cannot yet do, the layer that fixes most of it, and what it should never do

**Extends:** all prior addenda. **Brief:** review the whole stack against ACOUP and the wider military-history literature and find the gaps. The brief's own probe is the sharpest available: *can we watch a force lose cohesion as it marches dispersed, raids and pillages its discipline away, bleeds deserters — and then need it to cohere for tomorrow's march or battle?*

**The honest headline: no, not yet — and the probe found the design's thinnest layer.** We built the battle (minutes) in obsessive depth and the season (months) in structural depth. The **day** — the march, the camp, the forage circuit, the sack, the deserter on the night road, the sick list — exists in the current documents as a supply number ticking and a belief channel drifting. That is a summary of the operational day, not a simulation of it, and nearly every gap the audit found lives there. This addendum specifies the missing layer, then closes with the things the system still can't do and a judgment on whether it should.

---

## 1 — The audit, compressed

What holds up: the battle resolver (validated), command and officers (K), logistics as *consumption* (supply field, burn clock), battle-rarity and consent, casualties and pursuit, prisoners and ransom, the belief/cohesion split, the commission frame. What the literature says is missing, in rough order of severity: the **march as a physical process** (Devereaux's logistics-on-the-move material is barely used); **plunder and the sack** as discipline events (we price atrocities in ledgers but don't simulate the loss of control that produces them); **desertion as a daily flow** (we only have term-expiry); **disease** (flagged open since Addendum D, still open, and it killed more soldiers than battle did); the **camp** as anything but a Direction-A noun; and **meeting engagements** — battles that begin before armies finish becoming armies, which our battle-by-consent framing quietly assumes away.

One observation organizes the fix: these are all the same gap. The lattice coheres and percolates in *minutes*; the cloud concentrates and disperses in *days* — and we never specified the day-scale cohesion physics, even though the design's own unification principle (the same variables at every zoom) demands it. An army on the march **is** a lattice at operational scale: a structure that holds men in mutual reach, decays under dispersal and temptation, and must be actively re-formed. The missing layer is that sentence, mechanized.

## 2 — The Day Layer

### a. Column physics — the march as a formation

The numbers are already in the literature and they are brutal in the best way. Devereaux's worked example: a modest pre-industrial army forms a column **6.6 miles long moving at 2.5 mph**, so the rear elements begin marching more than two and a half hours after the head — and the head must stop where the *last* man can reach by dark, because the army camps together; the whole organism moves at the speed of its slowest element, while men fall out down its length — sick, lame, or faking — which is why the medics march at the rear. Civil War rules of thumb put 80 wagons to a mile of road; rear units could spend eighteen hours covering twenty miles; and Sherman marched on a **sixty-mile-wide front** in part to forage and in part simply to relieve road congestion.

Mechanized: army size × road class → column length, clearing time, and the day's true reach; the cloud's strands stop being abstract probability and become *scheduled columns* with heads, tails, and trailing straggler density. Everything downstream falls out. Multi-road marching ("march divided, fight united") becomes the only way large armies move at speed, which is Direction B's cloud re-derived from wagon math. A defile or bridge becomes a day-long bottleneck an enemy can read from the dust. And the **meeting engagement** — the gap our consent framing hid — emerges naturally: when columns touch, each side fights with whatever fraction has arrived and shaken out from column into line, the arrival problem from Addendum D now generated by the march system instead of asserted by the scenario file.

### b. The camp cycle — the re-cohesion tick

The brief's question — *cohere in time* — gets its mechanic here. Each night the army either re-forms or doesn't. Camp is where stragglers are gathered (a job belonging to **roles** — provosts, serjeants, optiones — so Addendum K's role-keyed capability now governs the operational day too: an army without rear-rank officer roles re-coheres slowly or not at all), where fatigue and order recover, where the sick are counted, and where the entrenching question is asked: the Romans marched heavy and dug nightly, buying security with daily labor — a fatigue-versus-safety trade the engine already knows how to price. A harassed camp, a dry camp, a camp pitched after dark because the column ran long: each denies part of the re-cohesion tick, and a commander who marches his army hard for four days is watching tomorrow's battle losses accumulate tonight, in the camp ledger, before any enemy appears.

### c. Forage circuits — the dispersal engine

Foraging is not a supply modifier; it is the army *taking itself apart on purpose*, daily. Parties fan out on a radius with diminishing local returns, under escort or not, and the circuit is simultaneously: the supply system's intake, the smallest and most common combat surface in the game (the thirty-man granary fight the resolver was built to make cheap), the desertion system's open door, and the discipline regime's daily examination. The operational-cohesion state is largely a function of how much of the army is out on circuits at any hour — which makes the brief's scenario a *readable dial*: a chevauchée deliberately runs the dial high for profit and pays for it in order, and the player watches the trade on the sand table as blocks that are smeared instead of square.

### d. Desertion as a flow

Term-expiry (the 40-day clocks) was never the whole story; desertion is a *daily hazard per man*, and every modulator has a receipt: the distance-home gradient (an army marching away from its men's homes leaks faster, and the harvest pulls levies like a tide), pay arrears (the bond edge to the chest, fraying), the belief channel, the discipline regime's enforcement receipts (provosts, exemplary punishments, pay regularity — a price list, not a slider), and **opportunity** — which is where the layer's systems interlock, because opportunity is mostly *dispersed duty*: men desert from forage circuits and night pickets, not from the middle of a marching column. Deserters then don't vanish from the simulation. They become density on the road graph — banditry the campaign's allegiance map must price, and information leaks, because the man who walked away from your camp knows your numbers and your hunger and will trade both for bread. The enemy's belief DB sharpening for free is the part of desertion no strategy game has ever charged for.

### e. Plunder and the sack — the discipline solvent

The hardest gap, and the engine already owns the physics: **a sack is a rout that runs toward instead of away** — the same percolation, the same loss of the authority graph, with greed and release in place of terror. The historical record is unambiguous about the dynamics. At Badajoz, once the storm succeeded after appalling losses, discipline collapsed with the sudden release of tension; *every* attempt to restore order failed, fresh troops sent in to stop the sack joined it instead, and it ended after some seventy-two hours essentially because the men ran out of energy — with soldiers threatening and even killing their own officers who tried to intervene. Wellington's own dispatches had already named the deeper coupling years earlier: a starving army is worse than none, because hunger dissolves discipline and spirit together — supply and order are one ledger. And the customary law that framed it all: a garrison that refused surrender after a practicable breach forfeited protection, making the sack "lawful" by the conventions both sides knew — which is why most sieges ended in negotiated capitulation precisely *under the shadow of that rule*.

Mechanized: the storm's success flips the assaulting force into the sack state — authority edges go dark on a timer governed by the same appraisal machinery (officers can carve out islands of order at personal risk, exactly as the sources describe individual officers doing); the duration runs on exhaustion, not command; the aftermath is days of operational incapacity ("disorganization from the plundering and loss of officers," as Wellington's army was described), loot with literal mass slowing the column, and entries in the atrocity and allegiance ledgers the campaign already keeps. The player's verbs all live *before* the breach: the summons-and-terms negotiation (the matrix ledger again — most sieges should end here, as they did), the discipline regime bought in advance (prize-money systems versus customary free plunder: a receipts choice with century-appropriate options), where the provosts stand, and whether to storm at all knowing that victory costs you your army's obedience for three days in a war where three days is a river crossing. The sack becomes the design's darkest honest mechanic: the moment the game takes the army away from you *because you won*.

### f. Disease — the true killer

Open since Addendum D; closed now. A camp-epidemiology model on the receipts the period actually offers: density × duration × water source × sanitation doctrine × season → a sick-list flow that dwarfs battle losses whenever an army sits still, which makes the burn clock a *double* clock — the stationary army starves and sickens on the same calendar, and sieges become the epidemiological races they were, with the besieger's camp often the worse city. The validation receipt is already in our own battery: Henry V's army lost a substantial fraction of its strength to dysentery at Harfleur *before* the Agincourt march — meaning our Agincourt scenario's English numbers and fatigue have a disease backstory the campaign layer should be able to generate, and "reproduce the Harfleur sick-clock, then march the survivors to our existing battle" becomes the Day Layer's end-to-end acceptance test.

## 3 — The probe, re-run

The brief's scenario through the new layer: your column burns a valley for a week — circuits wide, the dial high, blocks smeared across twenty miles. Each night the camp tick under-recovers because the army is rich, drunk, and scattered; each morning the muster rolls are shorter, and the road behind you is growing bandits who used to be yours. On the eighth day a rider: the enemy's cloud has concentrated and is two days off, offering battle behind the river. Now the design's question is the commander's question, with mechanics under every word: *can this army be an army again in two days?* The answer is computable and visible — stragglers gathered at the rate your provost roles allow, circuits recalled at rider speed, the loot question (abandon the wagons and the men's pay, or march slow and arrive late), the cohesion number climbing on the sand table as the blocks square up — or not. Caught at sixty percent coherence, you fight the meeting engagement from column. That is the game the probe asked for, and after this addendum it is specified rather than implied.

## 4 — What it still can't do, and whether it should

**Should, later:** naval and riverine warfare (rivers enter *now* as logistics arteries — water transport's cost advantage is too historically decisive to defer — but fleet combat is a second validation battery for after v1); volley-era linear warfare beyond the 1879 skirmish frame (the schema holds, the drill files and a new battery don't exist yet); full occupation and insurgency dynamics (the allegiance map and deserter-bandits give the substrate; a real guerrilla opponent is an expansion's worth of design); night and weather at battle resolution (queued since D).

**Shouldn't, by design:** the grand-strategic political economy — the Paradox layer — stays out, because the commission frame exists precisely so the world can use the player instead of the player painting the world; rank-and-file interiority beyond appraisal (no dialogue trees for privates — the floor is the floor); and **atrocity rendering**: the system simulates the *loss of control* and prices the consequences in every ledger it keeps, and it will never depict the contents of a sack beyond the chronicle's period-appropriate reticence. That is a values choice, stated once and kept: the game must be honest that these things happened and mechanically honest about why, and it owes no one the spectacle.

**Small and worth it:** Delbrück as a feature — the chronicle should inflate enemy numbers the way the sources do, with the Archive's trace as the corrective, so the game teaches source criticism by being a source.

**Verdict:** the audit found one structural gap wearing six costumes, and the Day Layer closes it with the design's own unification principle — cohesion physics at the third zoom. **Punchlist:** the march-state object (column schedule, straggler density, coherence dial); camp-tick spec with role-keyed recovery; forage-circuit system; desertion-flow hazards + deserter-bandit and information-leak consequences; sack state with pre-breach verbs and post-sack incapacity; camp epidemiology; validation battery additions — the Harfleur clock, a Badajoz sack test, a chevauchée fortnight, a Sherman march-width comparison, and a meeting-engagement-from-column scenario.
