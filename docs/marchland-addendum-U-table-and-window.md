# MARCHLAND — Addendum U: The Table and the Window
### The full-production render question — what this looks like at Unity/Unreal scale, and how it stands out in a lane Total War already owns

**Extends:** H (the three rooms) and I (the chronicler's eye), which answered architecture and POV. This answers the brief's escalation: if we go all the way to a fully rendered game, what is it — Mount & Blade embodiment? Kingdom Come groundedness? Isometric? The market context first, because it settles half the argument.

---

## 1 — The lane check

The photoreal mass-battle lane is *crowded and getting worse*: Creative Assembly's incumbency, Bannerlord's decade of embodied battles, and now a UE5 challenger (Chronicles: Medieval, 2,000 soldiers a map, entering early access) joining a tabletop-realism RTT (Strategos) in the same queue. Entering that lane means competing on asset budgets against studios that have already paid them. Meanwhile the *painterly* register is commercially hot — Heroes: Olden Era riding a million wishlists partly on its painted look; Wartales praised for it; even Total War's most celebrated recent art direction is Three Kingdoms' classical-Chinese-painting touches — but the heat is all on map layers and tactics screens. **Nobody has taken period art to the mass battle itself, and nobody has solved the god-camera problem with a diegetic grammar.** Those are the two white spaces, and the recommendation below occupies both at once.

---

## 2 — Four directions, evaluated against the design's own laws

**A. Embodied photoreal (the M&B / KCD register).** What it proves: Kingdom Come's success demonstrates a mass appetite for grounded historical authenticity, and Mount & Blade proves audiences forgive jank for systems — both encouraging. What it teaches us to *take*: the ground-level camera grammar — dust banks, sightlines, the bodily smallness of one rider before a moving mass — which is exactly the Knot seat's language. Why it cannot be the identity: that camera is a genre signal that promises avatar swordplay, the one verb this design refuses; shipping a KCD-looking game where you don't fight is a marketing self-wound, and shipping it photoreal puts us in §1's lane war. **Verdict: steal the camera grammar; refuse the genre signal.**

**B. Isometric / oblique (the Manor Lords register).** Strengths are real: maximum lattice legibility (the readable formation body-language the engine computes is *easiest to read* from oblique-above), the strategy audience's home angle, and a budget an order below full 3D fidelity — Manor Lords sold millions substantially on this look from a tiny team. The trap is fatal if unhandled: an isometric camera is the god camera in a flat cap, cursor-omniscience through the back door, the exact thing Addenda G–I dismantled. The redemption is the move this project has been circling since the sand table appeared: **make the isometric view diegetic.** You are not above the battle; you are above the *model* of the battle — the belief database rendered as living miniature. **Verdict: adopted, transformed — this is the Table.**

**C. Period-art NPR at full production (Addendum I, industrialized).** The thesis taken to engine: real-time non-photoreal rendering of the 3D sim in each era's own visual language — embroidery-flat 1066, byōbu-screen Sengoku, engraved 1879. Feasibility is a solved research space, not a bet: Okami did 3D ukiyo-e two decades ago; *11-11: Memories Retold* shipped real-time painterly NPR; Ghost of Tsushima's Kurosawa mode proved era-styled rendering as a celebrated feature; Return of the Obra Dinn proved total stylistic commitment *is* marketing. The known hard problem is motion coherence (painterly styles shimmer under camera movement), and it is hard-but-shipped, not open research. **Verdict: adopted — this is the Window's skin.**

**D. Pure 2D (the Banner Saga register).** Cheapest and most beautiful per dollar, but side-on 2D cannot carry the design's operational geometry — envelopment, bearing spread, the horns closing — which are inherently planar facts; tilt 2D up to oblique and it converges to B. **Verdict: folded into the Table's craft, not a direction of its own.**

---

## 3 — The recommendation: one sim, two renders

**THE TABLE.** Always present, the command space at every zoom: campaign and battle alike rendered as a *physical miniature world* — the sand table from Addendum I, now the production's primary visual surface. Its honesty trick is structural: the Table renders the **belief DB, never the world**, so its omniscience is the legitimate kind — god over your model, with the model's flaws on display. And its signature technique, which no game has shipped: **uncertainty as finish.** A rumored enemy column is a few rough charcoal-sketch blocks; a scouted one is unpainted lead; a confirmed one is a painted miniature; intelligence going stale is *dust gathering on the piece*, four days unread. The player learns to feel epistemic confidence as material finish — fog of war as craftsmanship, the whole reflexivity thesis made into an art style. Orders are physical: a piece slid, a note sealed, a rider miniature dispatched across the table that *is* the latency made visible.

**THE WINDOW.** The witness space: seat-locked 3D in the era's NPR skin, opened only when you are somewhere — the Knot on its hill, the Front Rank's three meters, the Archive's earned free flight. The render LOD *mirrors the sim tiers*, which is the production insight that makes it affordable: full skeletal animation only near the camera (Field tier), vertex-animated instanced crowds in the middle distance (Lattice tier), and the far masses as *painted density* — which is not a compromise but the style, because handling distant masses as stylized pattern is what period battle art always did, and gold-leaf cloud banks over the unseen wing are literal fog of war wearing Momoyama gold. Sound runs first-class here (H's truth-audio): the Window is as much heard as seen.

**THE DIVE.** The grammar binding them, and the trailer shot: lean into the Table and the camera falls into the miniature — the painted piece blooming into the Window at that piece's vantage — *available only where your knowledge permits*. You can dive onto your own hill; you cannot dive into a charcoal sketch. Kessen's zoom and the map-dive of cinema, disciplined by the epistemics: the transition itself teaches the game's central law every time it plays. Post-battle, the Archive unlocks the Window everywhere, with Addendum I's two-layer truth (the era's depiction versus the trace) as the scrub.

---

## 4 — Engine and production notes

The Addendum F architecture survives intact and pays off here: the **headless Rust core** owns simulation and determinism; Unity or Unreal is a *pure client* playing back trace streams — the renderer never needs deterministic physics, replays are free, and the engine choice becomes a tooling preference rather than a foundation bet (Unity's ECS/DOTS and GPU instancing comfortably handle the crowd counts; Bannerlord-scale skeletal counts near-camera, instanced thousands beyond, painted density past that — and our far tier is *cheaper* than the incumbents' because paint costs less than polygons). The real cost center is the one named in I and unchanged: the **art bible per era** — the NPR shader stack (ink, hatch, gouache, thread) is built once, but each era's visual language needs an art director's year, which is why one era ships first and the second exists to prove the pipeline general. UI law throughout: **no floating bars, ever** — unit state reads as body language and banner behavior; instruments are diegetic (the sun is the clock, the paper accumulating on the Table is the casualty report); and confidence-as-finish replaces every minimap and intel overlay the genre has trained players to expect.

---

## 5 — Standing out

Don't enter the UE5 Total-War-challenger queue; one is already in it and the incumbent is twenty years deep. The shelf presence this direction buys instead: a strategy game whose screenshots are *mistakable for period art and nothing else on the store page* — the tapestry that moves — and whose signature interaction, the dive from miniature to vantage, demonstrates the entire design philosophy in four wordless seconds. KCD2's lesson is that the historical-authenticity appetite is enormous; our claim on it is authenticity of *epistemics*, not just assets — the first war game where what you see is, rigorously, what someone could see, painted the way that someone's world painted war. The Kessen comparison the brief opened with becomes the elevator pitch's kicker: Kessen made a movie you could slightly control; this makes a *primary source you can stand inside.*

**Verdict:** the Table and the Window — diegetic miniature omniscience over your beliefs, seat-locked period-art witness of the world, the knowledge-gated dive between them — on a headless core with the engine as client. **Punchlist:** the NPR shader-stack technical spike (motion coherence test on a Lattice trace) before any renderer scoping; uncertainty-as-finish prototyped on the CLI's successor first (a 2D Table is the vertical slice's natural graduation); the dive storyboarded as the trailer; crowd-LOD plan mapped one-to-one onto sim tiers; the no-floating-bars law into the UI bible; era-one art-direction decision (1066 or Sengoku) scheduled with the slice.
