# MARCHLAND — Addendum M: The Loops, and the Judgment of Men
### What the player actually does for fun — and the officer question answered: promotion as the game's mastery axis

**Extends:** Addenda F–L. **Status note:** the Day Layer (L) is parked as agreed — queued for after the battle system reaches satisfaction, which was the build order anyway. This addendum inventories the gameplay loops the existing design already contains, then takes the brief's probe head on: should the player hold the power to promote officers and assign tasks, and should it matter as a test of discernment against cultural expectations? (Short answer: yes, and it may be the game's deepest loop. The long answer is §3.)

---

## 1 — The loop inventory, by clock speed

The design turns out to contain nested loops at five timescales, each with its own tension-release shape. Listing them honestly also reveals where enjoyment is thin, so this is an audit as much as a celebration.

**Seconds-to-minutes (in battle): the triage loop and the watch loop.** Proposals and reports arrive at rider latency; you approve, deny, redirect, or — the underrated verb — *ignore*, and every choice is made against information you know is stale. Between dispatches, the watch: reading the lattice's body language, the dread of a wing that has gone quiet, the specific relief of seeing file-closers seal a tear. The loop's pleasure is the air-traffic-controller pleasure — bounded attention under arriving pressure — except the controller can also choose to ride toward the problem, which converts an information decision into a mortal one (the station system as a panic button with a price). The thin spot: idle minutes must be *tense*, not empty; the sound channel and dispatch rhythm (Addendum H) carry this, and it is the first thing the CLI playtests must prove.

**Per battle: the plan-poker loop.** Forecast → council → deployment → the few commitments → the chronicle. The shape is a bet: you wager weeks of preparation (the receipts you bought, the ground you maneuvered onto, the plan you sold to the magnates) against irreducible uncertainty, and the chronicle pays out — in a currency that includes *being right*. A wrinkle the engine makes possible: the pre-battle forecast is your **staff's** assessment, generated from their belief DB and their competence — while the Archive later reveals the ensemble's true odds. The gap between what your marshal predicted and what was actually likely is itself information about your marshal, which feeds the loop in §3.

**Per season: the dealt-hand loop.** The commission deals an army, a mission, and strings; the satisfaction is the roguelike's — playing a hand well rather than holding a good one — plus the audit's drama, because the patron judges from *their* belief DB and the verdict can be unjust in both directions. Credit for a victory you know was luck; blame for a defeat the Archive will eventually show was decided before you arrived. Injustice as a renewable narrative resource.

**Per career: the legacy loop.** The staff you grew, the body you spent, the memoir compiling itself. Attachment mechanics do the heavy lifting here — the XCOM effect, named men whose files you have read for ten seasons — and the career's end-states give the whole stack a direction of travel. The thin spot: aging and scars must *bite* (the Front Rank growing genuinely expensive) or the career is a number going up.

**The meta-loop: learning the engine.** Because outcomes are mechanistic and traced, the player's model of warfare actually improves — the game teaches the Devereaux corpus by consequence rather than by tooltip. The Archive's forensics (death certificates, the counterfactual slider, the chronicle-versus-trace gap) make *study* a gameplay verb, the way Dwarf Fortress legends and Football Manager's analytics screens became destinations in their own right. This loop is the one no competitor can copy, because it requires the engine to have nothing to hide.

**Cross-cutting enjoyment elements**, named so the interface budget knows where to go: anticipation (the rider you can see coming before you can hear him); vindication (the called flank, confirmed); attachment (files, faces, funerals); earned spectacle (the Page); blame and credit as drama (the audit, the court); and expression — which is currently the thinnest element of all, and gets its long-term answer at the end of §3.

## 2 — The precedent that proves the probe

Before designing the officer game, name the existence proof: **Football Manager.** One of the most durable franchises in the medium is, mechanically, *exactly* the brief's proposal — drop a person into an organization full of subordinates; let them judge competence from records, scout reports of varying reliability, and watched performances they do not control; let them promote, assign roles, and live with the consequences; make the watching itself (matches they cannot pilot) the payoff screen. FM proves that judging people and then watching your judgment be tested is a genre-carrying loop, not a niche garnish. What MARCHLAND adds is the part FM structurally cannot have, and it changes everything: **ground truth.** FM's hidden attributes are authored numbers; our officers have no numbers at all, only records generated by a mechanistic engine — which means the game *knows*, via the ensemble and the trace, whether Aldric's famous victory was skill or a 4-in-100 seed, and can eventually let the player find out. Discernment becomes not just a feeling but a *scoreable, improvable skill*. No management game has ever been able to make that offer honestly.

## 3 — The judgment of men: promotion and assignment as the mastery axis

So: yes to the probe, on all three clauses — the player should hold the power, it should be a test of discernment, and the cultural-expectations layer is what elevates it from a staffing minigame to the game's deepest system. The design:

**The evidence is files, never numbers.** Everything Addendum K established now pays off. The player judges from service records, proposals made and acted-on, the forecast-versus-outcome gaps of §1, what other officers' trust ledgers imply (who follows whom willingly is observable), and watched behavior in battles. All of it is *noisy in the exact ways historical judgment was noisy*: outcomes conflate luck and skill; records are written by chroniclers with sympathies; a man's reputation among his peers measures his legibility as much as his competence. The Archive's delayed ground truth turns this from frustration into a learnable craft — the player who reviews last season's traces gets *measurably better at reading men*, and the game can quietly show them their own attribution errors, which is a humility no other genre delivers.

**The power itself is culturally variable — that's the second test hiding inside the first.** What "promote" means is a class D fact: in the Roman frame you genuinely appoint (the centurionate is an institutional ladder and your discretion on it is real), but the tribunes arrive dealt by politics; in the feudal frame you cannot promote the Earl or demote him — precedence is birth — and your lever is the assignment of *honors*: the vanguard, the right of the line, who holds the bridge, each politically radioactive; in the purchase-system frame, merit and money openly bid against each other and the player feels the system's famous absurdity from inside; in the Napoleonic frame, the career open to talent makes merit-promotion the *norm*, and a player arriving from a feudal campaign experiences the difference as liberation with its own new failure modes. And everywhere, the rare rank-jump — the sandal-bearer raised to command, the serjeant knighted on the field — exists as a high-cost, high-yield exception whose price is paid in every other officer's trust ledger.

**Competence and promotability are different axes, and the gap between them is the gameplay.** The brilliant commoner serjeant in a feudal host *cannot* be given the wing — or rather, he can, and the magnates' trust ledgers, the levies' appraisal of an illegible commander, and your patron's audit will invoice you for it; sometimes the invoice is worth paying, and knowing when is the discernment test at full depth. Conversely the mediocre nephew must be given *something*, and the craft is finding the assignment where his deficits are cheapest — garrison command, the baggage, an honor that flatters without deciding anything. The player isn't solving "find the best man." The player is solving *staff the army your culture permits, and spend political capital exactly where merit matters most* — which is the actual problem every historical commander had, and which no stat-sorted interface has ever let a player feel.

**Assignment failure modes come from the station and role systems, free.** The lion of the front rank promoted to the Hill, whose priors were forged three meters from the enemy and who cannot read a field he isn't bleeding on. The superb administrator who anchors nothing — men do not rate him, and his wing's cohesion shows it. The trusted veteran given the rearguard (the dangerous honor) one season too many. None of these need a single new mechanic: priors, anchor radii, role-keyed repertoires, and trust ledgers already produce them; the officer game is *configuration of systems we have validated*, which is the same happy sentence Addendum J got to write about stations.

**Development closes the career loop.** Experience writes priors, assignments write records, your tutelage is itself an input (the officer who served under your doctrine carries it — repertoire literally propagates through people, which is how drill actually transmitted), and the staff you built becomes the thing the world takes from you: poached by rivals, promoted away by the patron, killed by the lottery you assigned them to. Losing your best officer to your own success is the career game's signature wound.

**And the expression problem gets its long-term answer here.** The thinnest enjoyment element in §1 was expression — and its endgame is the officer system matured: a player whose innovations (the rehearsed feint, the relief rotation, the forecast discipline) have propagated through enough officers can, late in a career, *codify them* — author a doctrine file in the closed vocabulary, found a school, become the receipt that future armies purchase. The player as Maurice of Nassau is the expansion-tier fantasy this design has quietly been pointing at since Addendum A made doctrine a data file.

## 4 — Risks

The officer game must not become a spreadsheet wearing a doublet: the file UI, the proposal rhythm, and hard caps on span-of-control are the existing mitigations, and the new one is *pacing* — promotion moments should be scarce, seasonal, ceremonial (the winter court), so each carries weight. The attribution loop can curdle into hindsight torture if the Archive shames the player too eagerly; ground truth should be available, never shoved. And cultural pricing must never read as the game endorsing the gatekeeping it simulates — the feudal frame's refusal of the brilliant commoner is presented as the system's cost, visible in the ledgers, sometimes worth defying; the game's sympathies live in the receipts, as ever.

**Verdict:** the loops exist at five clock speeds and the thin spots are named (idle tension, aging bite, expression). The probe is adopted in full: promotion and assignment as the discernment game, with cultural expectations as its second layer and ensemble ground truth as its unprecedented teaching mechanism. **Punchlist:** forecast-vs-outcome tracking per officer; appointment/honor slots in the culture schema; promotion ceremonies in the winter court; rank-jump exception mechanics with trust-ledger pricing; tutelage/doctrine propagation through served-under records; the attribution-review screen in the Archive (gentle by default); the doctrine-authoring endgame flagged for expansion scope.
