# MARCHLAND — Addendum N: The Seven Players
### Audience personas against a design that breaks their habits — and the modularity audit

**Extends:** Addenda F–M. **Brief:** who actually plays this, what will they love and resent given habits formed by conventional strategy games, and is the architecture modular enough to expand? The persona work below is deliberately honest about friction — a design this opinionated must name its non-audience as clearly as its core — and each persona ends with a verdict: *core, reachable, deferred,* or *not for them.*

One cross-cutting truth first, because every persona below collides with it: traditional strategy games train three habits this design withdraws — **control** (units obey instantly), **information** (fog is a toggle, the map is true), and **attribution** (losses trace to visible numbers). The withdrawal is the thesis, so it cannot be softened mechanically; it can only be made *legible*. The design's unique anti-frustration technology is its transparency: the engine cannot cheat, the Archive shows its work, and every loss comes with receipts. Where a conventional game answers frustration with rubber-banding, we answer it with explanation. Whether that trade lands is, persona by persona, the whole question.

---

## 1 — The Field Marshal (Total War veteran)

Wants mass spectacle and tactical authorship; has won a thousand battles with a cursor. **Hooks:** the deployment chess (which the TW multiplayer scene already knows is where battles are won), the Saddle's dread, the Archive — which hands them the free camera and the cinematic replay they came for, *after*. **Frictions:** the deepest of any persona. Orders that travel, units that decline, idle minutes, and above all the ensemble's verdict that a defeat was structural — "the game says I never had a chance" reads as an insult until literacy arrives. This persona produces the review that says *the AI plays the game for you*. **Accommodations without compromise:** onboarding that frontloads the forecast and chronicle so preparation *feels* like the control it actually is; the Archive positioned as theirs from the first hour; stale-vignette cinematics scratching the spectacle itch mid-battle. **Verdict: reachable, in two halves** — the deployment-minded half converts and becomes loud about it; the piloting-minded half is honestly *not for them*, and marketing should never promise them otherwise.

## 2 — The Chancellor (Paradox grand strategist)

Map-painters, ledger-readers, character-drama collectors. **Hooks:** the receipts economy is the EU4 ledger with a soul; the belief DB is fog of war finally taken seriously; the court interludes, officer files, and unjust audits are CK3's character drama with mechanical teeth. **Frictions:** the commission frame *removes map-painting* — you are used by the world, you do not own it — and the one-army scope reads as a demo of the game they imagined. **Accommodations:** the grand-campaign mode (Directions A/B at full scale) is the architecture's planned second act, and this persona is its pre-sold audience; until then, the CK3-shaped half engages fully through the career and court while the EU4-shaped half is **deferred** — honestly told the state-game comes later, on the same engine.

## 3 — The Kriegsspieler (grognard, milsim, courier-play veteran)

Already plays this game wherever fragments of it exist — Scourge of War's headquarters mode, Radio Commander, hex wargames, Dominions. The Addendum H research found them defending interface opacity *on principle*. **Hooks:** everything; this is the audience the validation battery was secretly written for, and they will read Addendum E for fun. **Frictions:** two, both aesthetic — the period-art stylization may scan as unserious to a community that tolerates ugliness and distrusts beauty, and the protagonist/career frame may scan as "gamey" to players who want the staff exercise straight. **Accommodations:** a sandbox/scenario mode without the career wrapper costs almost nothing (the harness already is one), and the art direction's legibility-first argument should be made to them explicitly. **Verdict: core.** Small, loud, evangelical, and the correct early-access population.

## 4 — The Gaffer (Football Manager, OOTP)

The largest adjacent audience nobody in the genre is courting. **Hooks:** Addendum M is their loop verbatim — judge people from files, assign roles, watch outcomes you don't control, live in the blame economy — plus the ground-truth twist FM can never offer. **Frictions:** the spatial battle layer intimidates; the military-historical setting is unfamiliar territory for a persona raised on league tables. **Accommodation — and it's already a mechanic:** the station system contains an FM mode natively. Appoint a field commander, take the Camp station, and the battle arrives as dispatches while you manage the organization — *delegation as a legitimate, culturally priced playstyle*, not a difficulty setting. **Verdict: reachable**, and strategically precious, because they normalize the watch-don't-pilot covenant for everyone else.

## 5 — The Chronicler (RimWorld, CK3-anecdote, King of Dragon Pass)

Plays for the stories the system generates and the people it kills. **Hooks:** named men with files and funerals, the memoir compiling itself, injustice as drama, the chronicle that lies the way sources lie. RimWorld proved the story-generator audience is enormous and tolerant of depth *if the stories surface themselves*. **Frictions:** military jargon and operational failure-spirals can gate the stories behind competence; a career that ends in three seasons of confusion generates no memoir worth keeping. **Accommodations:** the chronicle generator's prose quality is *for them* (already promoted to core in J); survivable-failure defaults (capture and ransom over death); a "patient patron" opening commission that forgives the learning seasons. **Verdict: reachable**, and the source of the screenshots that sell the game.

## 6 — The Condottiere (Battle Brothers, Darkest Dungeon, run-based)

Wants the dealt hand, the brutal economy, the named roster ground down by an indifferent world. **Hooks:** the commission loop *is* a run structure; capture-and-ransom is the most elegant run-continuation mechanic in the genre (failure makes you poorer, not deader); the aging clock is the meta-progression. Battle Brothers proved this audience will learn deep systems for the privilege of being hurt by them. **Frictions:** pacing — runs want crisp loops, and a season has slow middles; ironman purists will want the *promachoi* bargain at full lethality. **Accommodations:** both already specified (ironman toggle, mission-length variety). **Verdict: core.**

## 7 — The Student (the ACOUP reader)

Exists as a presold market in a way almost no game audience does: the critique this design answers has its own readership, and they have been asking "why doesn't a game do this" in comment sections for years. **Hooks:** the meta-loop — the game as the executable footnotes of the corpus they already read; the Archive's forensics; the Delbrück feature; the validation battery published as content. **Frictions:** nearly none with the design; some with *games* — a portion of this persona doesn't play strategy titles and needs the onboarding to assume no genre fluency. **Verdict: core**, and the marketing wedge — the rare launch where the most credible reviewer pool was the design's bibliography.

(An eighth persona — the competitive multiplayer duelist — is named only to be **deferred honestly**: symmetric-information competition contradicts the thesis, and the right multiplayer is the asymmetric, asynchronous play-by-dispatch mode in §3, which is its own future.)

## 2 — What the personas teach the design

Reading the seven together yields three product rules. **First, the covenant must be on the box:** *you command, you do not pilot* — every persona's worst experience is discovering the withdrawal of control as a surprise; every persona's best is choosing it. **Second, difficulty is never simulation handicap:** the engine cannot cheat in either direction (no rubber-band morale for the struggling, no AI bonuses for the skilled), so difficulty lives entirely in the dealt hand — commission harshness, patron fairness, staff quality, lethality toggles. This is both a constraint and the most defensible difficulty design in the genre: the world gets harder, the physics never lie. **Third, the stations are secretly the persona system:** the Gaffer lives at Camp, the Field Marshal at the Hill, the Condottiere at the Knot, the ironman romantic in the Front Rank — the design already contains its own playstyle settings as diegetic, culturally priced choices, which is more elegant than any menu.

## 3 — The modularity audit

The brief's second question, answered axis by axis. The architecture was built data-first and tier-separated for *internal* reasons (the receipts audit, the fitting pipeline); the expansion surfaces fall out of those same decisions.

**The content axis — cultures and eras as data.** A "faction" is already a file bundle: doctrine vocabulary, station price list, authority-edge types and succession rules, career frame, art language, and a validation battery. That makes era expansions well-defined work with built-in QA: the gunpowder/linear-warfare era (flagged in L) is new drill files plus a new battery plus an art bible — *no engine fork* — and the receipts grep polices every content drop, first-party or modded. The same schema is the mod API; the Maurice-of-Nassau doctrine-authoring endgame (M) doubles as the modding tool shipping inside the game.

**The mode axis.** The commission game → the grand campaign (the Chancellor's second act, on the substrate that already exists) → and the multiplayer the design actually wants: **asynchronous play-by-dispatch** — two players, one war, orders and reports exchanged on real-world latency, the belief DB doing what it was born for. Play-by-mail reborn with an engine underneath; the dispatch interface makes async *diegetic* rather than a compromise. Deterministic mode (E) already provides the replay-integrity layer competitive async needs.

**The simulation axis.** Tier slots are open by construction: the Day Layer (parked, slot reserved, interfaces named in L), naval and riverine as a new resolver battery on the same schema, Field-tier flagship growth as hardware allows. The tier contract — shared variables, CI re-fitting — is what keeps additions from forking the model.

**The presentation axis.** The three rooms decouple cleanly: the CLI client persists forever as the accessibility and modder surface; the memoir export is shareable-content infrastructure (the Chronicler persona's screenshots, systematized); new room skins per era ride the art-language packs.

**The honest coupling risks.** Three, all manageable if respected: the Front tier silently staling when content changes (already a CI mandate — it becomes *load-bearing* the day mods exist); deterministic replays breaking across engine versions (replays must pin sim versions — a save-format decision to make on day one, alongside the fixed-point one); and art-language cost scaling with eras (the one-era launch discipline from I, held to).

**Verdict:** yes — modular by construction rather than by aspiration, because the anti-essentialist data schema and the tier contract were modularity all along; the expansion map writes itself as *content packs with batteries, the grand campaign, play-by-dispatch, and the parked Day Layer*, in roughly that order. **Punchlist:** the covenant statement as marketing canon; difficulty-as-dealt-hand spec; station-based onboarding paths per persona; sandbox/scenario mode for the Kriegsspieler; sim-version pinning in the save format; the mod-facing receipts validator packaged with the doctrine-authoring tools.
