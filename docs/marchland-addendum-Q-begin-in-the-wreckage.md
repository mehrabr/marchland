# MARCHLAND — Addendum Q: Begin in the Wreckage
### Rupture as the campaign frame — and an imagined ACOUP review of the whole project, written against ourselves

**Extends:** Addendum P's rupture family, now promoted from mechanic to *framing device*, per the brief: drop the player into the aftermath of a disaster — historical or contrived — with limited resources and a license windfall, to rebuild army institutions in their image. Part 2 is the second half of the brief: a pretend ACOUP article critiquing the game, written as an internal red-team exercise.

---

## Part 1 — The rupture start

### Why it solves four problems at once

The post-catastrophe opening is not just dramatic; it is load-bearing. It solves **scope** (a shattered army is a small army — the commission's one-army discipline arrives diegetically); **license** (Addendum O's hardest currency is granted as the scenario's premise: the old paradigm's defenders are dead, discredited, or captured, and reform is cheap for exactly as long as the wound is fresh); **the isekai** (the Maurice fantasy — rebuilding in your image — granted at the start instead of earned at the end, but priced by poverty and time); and **identity** (the campaign-select screen stops being "pick a faction" and becomes **pick a wound**, which no other game's menu has ever said).

### Anatomy of a rupture scenario

```
THE WOUND       the disaster itself, shipped as a playable Archive:
                the trace, the chronicle, the death certificates of
                the day everything broke — the player's first hour
                is FORENSIC, reading the catastrophe they inherit
THE DIAGNOSIS   the dossier the court argues over: what the trace
                says failed (and what the factions CLAIM failed —
                the two need not agree, and the gap is politics)
THE INHERITANCE survivors with scars and priors; institutions in
                fragments (roles unfilled, repertoires half-lost);
                a treasury, a patron in shock, a license windfall
                targeted at the diagnosed cause
THE CLOCK       the victor consolidating; the next test arriving
                whether the rebuild is ready or not
THE JUDGMENT    victory framed not as reconquest but as the new
                institution surviving its first real audit — the
                next battle, fought by the army you remade
```

The forensic opening deserves emphasis: for historical starts we *author the disaster's trace* — Cannae, Jena, Manzikert rendered as full Archive objects — so the player's tutorial is reading death certificates from the most studied catastrophes in military history, and their first decisions are positions taken in the diagnosis debate. The game teaches its own systems by autopsy.

### The launch slate

**Rome after Cannae (216 BC).** The canonical refusal: a state that loses an eighth of its adult male citizens in an afternoon and does not negotiate. The inheritance is the *legiones Cannenses* — survivors exiled to Sicily in disgrace, the most receipt-rich units imaginable (shame as a prior; redemption as a belief channel) — and the license windfall is Fabian: the strategy of refusal becomes politically speakable because annihilation discredited the battle-seekers. The rebuild test arrives years later, and the player decides whether their Rome reaches its Zama.

**France after Agincourt (1415).** The long rebuild with the best-documented institutional payoff in the medieval world: from feudal catastrophe to the *compagnies d'ordonnance* — Western Europe's first standing army — and the Bureau brothers' artillery train, vindicated at Formigny and Castillon. A career-length arc in which the player dismantles the exact military society our own Agincourt scenario simulates losing, against magnates whose trust ledgers correctly read the reform as their abolition.

**Prussia after Jena (1806).** The paradigm case from Addendum P, with its mythology handled honestly (see the article below): the staff system, the reformers, the army rebuilt under occupation's constraints — limited resources as treaty-capped troop numbers, which history solved with the Krümper rotation system, a mechanic begging to exist.

**Byzantium after Manzikert (1071).** The rebuild that mostly *failed* — included deliberately, because the slate must not promise that wreckage plus wisdom equals renaissance. Theme money where the mouth is.

**Britain after Isandlwana (1879).** The short-clock rupture on the *battery we already validated*: weeks, not decades — the column reformed, the laager doctrine adopted, the same enemy returning — and the player's rebuild audited at their Khambula or their second disaster.

**And the contrived slot, with the design's best trick: alt-history sampled from our own ensembles.** We never author counterfactual disasters as fanfic; we *promote seeds*. The 5-in-12 world where the Isandlwana square held is a validated possible history — so is its inverse, and so is the 2-in-100 Agincourt where the mud dried. Every alt-history rupture ships with its seed, its trace, and its ensemble context: "this happened in 13 of 100 worlds; you live in one of them." Alt-history with receipts — no other franchise can even define the genre this way.

### The anti-Whig guards

The brief's phrase — *rebuild in their image* — is the fantasy and also the trap Addendum P named: improvement-narrative with the player as history's direction. Three guards, all existing mechanics pointed at the problem: **rebuilds ossify** (path-dependent scars mean your new institution keeps its founding trauma's shape, and the game's later wars probe exactly the over-fit — the army magnificently prepared for the war it already lost); **rivals rebuild too** (the Red Queen does not pause for your montage; the victor is also drawing lessons from the same battle, often better positioned to apply them); and **the audit is the future, not your intentions** (the new model army is judged by its next war's trace, and the ensemble will say, without sentiment, whether your reforms or your enemy's mistakes won it). The player may rebuild in their image. History retains the right to find the image wanting.

## Part 2 — The imagined review

*What follows is pastiche, written by us against ourselves as a red-team exercise: an ACOUP-style critique imitating the blog's voice and conventions. It is fiction. Bret Devereaux has not seen this project and none of the views below should be attributed to him — the point is to borrow the most qualified imaginary reviewer we could construct and let him hit us where it hurts.*

---

> # Collections: A Wargame Reads the Syllabus, Part I: Marching Through MARCHLAND
>
> This week we're doing something a little unusual: looking at a strategy game design that, by its own documentation, was built *out of* the critiques this blog has spent years making about the genre — the omniscient general, the health-bar morale, the logistics that don't exist, the tech tree as Whig history with a research cost. Naturally I was going to read it; the documents cite me the way term papers cite Wikipedia, which is to say constantly and with occasional accuracy.¹
>
> And I want to be fair up front, because I am going to spend most of this post complaining: this is the first design I have seen that treats the problems as *load-bearing* rather than as flavor. Battles are rare and consensual. Armies live on a road network and starve on schedule. Morale is two channels of actual people instead of a meter. Orders travel at the speed of a frightened horse. The dead are mostly made in the pursuit. Someone has, at minimum, *done the reading*. The validation experiments — rerunning Isandlwana until the ammunition fails on the correct flank — are the kind of thing I'd assign if I could assign software.
>
> Now the complaining, in my customary numbered and excessive fashion.
>
> **First: the battery launders historiography as physics.** The design is rightly proud that its engine "takes positions" — Agincourt structure-decided, Hastings near-run. But *near-run according to whom?* Our sources for Hastings are thin, late, partisan, and literarily conventional; "the battle hung in the balance all day" is what chroniclers *say about battles*, the way every senator is "famed for eloquence." When the designers tuned their engine until Hastings became contested, they were not calibrating against the event — no one has access to the event — they were calibrating against the *Carmen* and William of Poitiers, plus nine centuries of narrative tradition with its thumb on the scale.² The documents half-know this (the chronicle-that-lies is their best feature), but the validation battery treats the historiographical consensus as ground truth while the rest of the design treats all sources as suspects. Pick one epistemology. My suggestion: publish each scenario's calibration with its *source-criticism*, ranges instead of targets, and let the battery wear the same humility the chronicle does. The Delbrück feature they propose for army numbers should apply to their own targets.
>
> **Second: the universal soldier is a modeling choice wearing a lab coat.** The "class A constants — identical for every human" doctrine is the design's anti-racist firewall, and I am genuinely sympathetic: the alternative, per-people coefficients, is how the genre has been laundering martial-race nonsense for forty years. But let's be clear about what is being claimed. Reaction-time physiology, fine. The *appraisal model* — what frightens men, what steadies them, how panic propagates — is a theory of mind, and declaring it species-universal is a position in a live debate, not a discovery.³ The honest framing is that universalism here is a *normative commitment with empirical costs*, chosen because the alternative's costs are worse. Say that in the documentation. The receipts audit guards against one failure mode; it does not make the substrate culture-free, it makes the substrate's culture *invisible*, which is the more dangerous version if nobody names it.
>
> **Third: you have rebuilt the battle fetish you set out to cure.** I have written, at length, that the pitched battle is the *rare* punctuation of pre-modern war and the siege its actual grammar — and the design agrees with me in its campaign layer and then spends five addenda, a validated prototype, and its entire art budget on... the pitched battle resolver. The siege remains "a hunger clock plus some escalade notes." The Day Layer — the march, the camp, the operational grammar of war — is *parked*. Friends: your build order is the genre's id speaking through you. The thing you built first and best is the thing you told us mattered least. I understand the engineering logic (the resolver is the hard science risk), but understand what it predicts: a launch where the battle is sublime and the siege is a menu, which is to say, Total War with better footnotes. The corrective is simple and painful — the siege gets a battery too, before the renderer gets a dollar.
>
> **Fourth: the protagonist problem, or, Carlyle at the stage door.** The design prices the great man beautifully — stations, lotteries, audits — and then the rupture campaigns hand the player Prussia-after-Jena and whisper *rebuild it in your image*. I note that the Jena-to-1813 story is itself partly a myth the reformers wrote about themselves,⁴ and a game that lets every player be Scharnhorst is teaching the myth with mechanics, which is the most persuasive way to teach anything. The guards (ossification, the Red Queen, failed-rebuild scenarios like Manzikert) are the right instinct. I'd go further: sometimes the diagnosis dossier should be *wrong* — the court's consensus reform aimed at a cause the trace does not support — so the player learns that institutions mostly reform around the lesson they can afford to learn, not the one the evidence taught.
>
> **Fifth, briefly, a list of quibbles in the classical style:** the game teaches its model, not history, and the documents' pride about the "meta-loop" should be tempered by every EU4 player who learned that Westphalia was about mana; the atrocity-as-ledger choice is defensible and the designers defend it, but a chevauchée whose victims appear as allegiance modifiers is still the command tent's view of a burning village, and the multi-vocal Archive needs to be more than *occasional* or it is a land acknowledgment; horses-as-agents delights me beyond reason and I demand the destrier training receipts be period-accurate; and whoever decided the chronicle should only say "a near-run thing" when the seeds agree has understood this blog better than some of my colleagues, which is either flattering or alarming.
>
> **Conclusions.** Is it good? The design is the most serious attempt I know of to make the academic critique of the genre *executable*, and its failures are interestingly located exactly where execution forced a choice the critique never had to make. That is what building does to criticizing, and I say this with the affection of a man who has chosen the safer trade. Buy it for the Archive; stay for the part where your patron blames you for the weather.
>

## What the exercise yielded

Stripped of costume, the pastiche found four corrections worth adopting: **(1)** scenario calibration must carry source-criticism — ranges and confidence, not point targets; the battery wears the chronicle's humility (battery historiography from P, extended). **(2)** The universalism doctrine gets reframed in the docs as a normative commitment with named empirical costs, not a discovered fact. **(3)** The siege gets promoted: a siege battery (summons-and-terms, the hunger race, one escalade) joins the validation suite *before* renderer spend — the build order amended to stop recapitulating the battle fetish. **(4)** Diagnosis dossiers can be wrong: rupture scenarios where the court's consensus mis-reads the trace, teaching that institutions learn the lessons they can afford.

**Verdict:** rupture adopted as the campaign frame — pick a wound — with the slate above, ensemble-promoted alt-history as the contrived slot's method, and the anti-Whig guards live. The imagined review goes in the design bible as standing red-team. **Punchlist:** the five-scenario slate with authored disaster Archives; the Krümper-style capped-rebuild mechanic; seed-promotion tooling for alt-history starts; source-criticism fields in scenario calibration; the universalism framing rewrite; the siege battery, scheduled ahead of the renderer; wrong-dossier rupture variants.
