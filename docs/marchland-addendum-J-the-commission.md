# MARCHLAND — Addendum J: The Commission
### A protagonist for the engine — stations of command priced by culture, and the campaign season as the chapter of a career

**Extends:** Addendum I's seats, which gave the player bodies. This addendum gives the bodies a *person*: an embodied general whose command venues are a culturally priced menu (the brief's instinct, and Devereaux's actual typology), and whose game is a career told season by season, in the Sid Meier's Pirates! register — constraints dealt, missions assigned, stories accumulated.

---

## 1 — Two ideas that are one idea

The brief proposes (a) visual-novel scenes for commanding from different venues — the front rank, behind the lines, the camp — matched to Devereaux's culture-based leadership expectations, and (b) embodying a general through campaign seasons, each opening with a dealt hand (the army your government spared, the mission your situation demands) and accumulating into a grand narrative like Pirates! built careers out of episodes.

These are the same move seen from two ends: **the game finally gets a protagonist.** Everything built so far — the engine, the seats, the receipts — has been a world without a "you." The station system is the protagonist's *body in battle*; the commission season is the protagonist's *life in time*. Together they answer the oldest open question in these documents (what is this, as a game someone plays on a Tuesday?) with: it is the story of a commander, told in seasons, in a world that refuses to flatter them.

---

## 2 — The stations: Devereaux's typology, mechanized

The Total Generalship series doesn't just critique the god-camera; it supplies the replacement menu. Devereaux's spectrum of pre-modern command runs from the **warrior-hero** — the Homeric *promachoi*, fore-fighters in or beyond the front ranks — through the **battle manager** in between (his examples: Pyrrhus, Alexander, Caesar — positioned to direct, fighting at chosen moments), to the **commander** who stays clear of the fighting and coordinates reserves, which he notes is *rare* in the pre-modern world precisely because cultures demanded otherwise. And the series' central claim about this spectrum: cultural ideals heavily constrain where on it a general may stand, especially when they demand he fight in person. Generals are not free to command however they like; they must *perform* generalship as their society defines it.

That is a station system with the prices already researched. Four stations, each a tuple the engine can already evaluate:

Every column is an existing mechanism wearing a new label: the anchor is the appraisal-anchor effect (Addendum B); the lottery is the leader-risk system the Hastings runs already fire (Harold fell in 7 of 12 seeds, and the cascade that followed is in the data); the latency is the dispatch system; "sees" is the belief DB scoped to a body. **The station system is configuration, not new simulation** — which is why it can be a launch feature rather than a dream.

The trade the stations encode is the one the sources keep narrating. Caesar at Pharsalus-era engagements dismounting to share his soldiers' peril — a morale gesture — and consequently, Devereaux notes, *possibly unaware* of a maneuver his own rear ranks executed without him: the heroic gesture **purchased belief with situational awareness**, and our Front Rank station must charge exactly that price. At the front you hold one lever and lose the other four, not as a penalty but as a fact: there is no battle from in there, only a fight. Conversely Caesar at Alesia, nearly fifty, donning the scarlet cloak and leading the counterattack *visibly* — the prepared, timed, dramatic personal intervention as a doctrine repertoire item, not a default. Riding forward mid-battle is a station change with travel time across a battlefield, a lottery en route, and an anchor payoff on arrival. The scarlet cloak is, mechanically, a signal the whole army can see: the strongest single appraisal event the player can author, spendable once.

**Culture writes the price list** — class D receipts, per the design law. In the Homeric and Macedonian frames, the Hill drains belief by the hour: the king who will not bleed is a cue in every soldier's appraisal, and Alexander's career is the system's tuning target (the battle manager who *must* periodically be the warrior-hero to remain legible as king). In the Roman frame the prices invert: the consul in the front rank reads as an army abandoned, while the Knot — riding the line, committing reserves, the cloak held in reserve for the crisis — is what *virtus* in command looks like. In the medieval frame the king's visible body in the press is constitutive of the host's cohesion (Hastings being the worked example the engine has already run, twelve times). And in the 1879 frame the prices have inverted again: the Camp is the *norm*, the colonel's place is with the maps, and a commander galloping to the firing line reads to his own staff as the line failing. Same engine, same stations, four different careers — because the price list is a data file.

**The visual-novel scenes are how stations are *rendered*** — the brief's instinct, exactly. Each station is a scene template with its own human texture: the Front Rank scene is faces and noise and one choice that cannot wait; the Knot scene is the staff's argument while a rider wheels away; the Hill scene is the landscape and the terrible luxury of seeing; the Camp scene is the Desk from Addendum I, now revealed as just the rearmost station of a single spectrum. The VN idiom carries what each venue *feels* like to a person; the engine underneath guarantees the feeling is priced.

## 3 — The commission: a season as a chapter

The second half of the brief reframes the campaign. You are not the state. You are a person the state *uses* — and each campaign season opens with the using:

**The commission deals the hand.** A patron (king, senate, daimyo, colonial office, a city's council) grants: an army you did not choose — the muster rolls as dealt cards, every receipt visible (these shires owed these days; the treasury could afford this much mail; the veterans went to the other commander, the one the chancellor likes); a mission from a small taxonomy that happens to be the main document's historicity tests wearing objectives: *intercept the invader before the harvest; raid the duchy for profit and ruin; take the fortress before the rivers rise; relieve the garrison; escort the convoy; hold the march through the season; put down the rising.* And strings: a deadline (the 40-day clocks now have a narrative face), a co-commander you didn't pick, a quarter policy imposed from above, a political officer whose dispatches home you cannot read.

**The season is the chapter structure the logistics model already enforces.** Muster (spring, the clocks start) → operations (the entire campaign game of Addenda F–G, played inside the commission's constraints) → resolution (the mission audited — by the patron, from *their* belief DB, which may not match what happened) → the winter interlude. The interlude is the court, in the visual-novel register the stations established: credit and blame argued to your face, ransom negotiations for the men you lost and the men you took, land or office or a marriage offered or withheld, rivals advancing, and the next season's commission negotiated from whatever standing this one left you. The matrix-game argument ledger (Addendum E, Mode 3) gets its second venue: the umpire's reasoning, now performed by courtiers about *you*.

**The Pirates! loop, made historically literal.** What persists across seasons is the person: fame, standing with patrons, wealth, scars (wounds from the lottery are carried — campaign-state receipts on your own body), a growing staff of named officers with their own appraisal priors about you, and the memoir. Defeat is not a game-over screen because the design already built the alternative: **a beaten general is a captured general, and the player enters the prisoner economy in person** — ransomed at a price that is the game's frank opinion of your market value, returning next season poorer and owing. Aging forces the arc Pirates! discovered: the body's class-A curves apply to you too, the front-rank station gets more expensive every year, and the career must end somewhere — the estates, the triumph, the senate's ingratitude, or a glorious and culturally correct death among the *promachoi*. The grand narrative the brief asks for is then not authored; it is **compiled**: the Page (Addendum I) has been writing the chronicle of every battle and the dispatches of every season, and the career's end binds them — a memoir in the era's own art and prose, which is the score screen, the share artifact, and the game's thesis stated one final time. Stories accumulated each season, exactly as briefed; the chronicle generator was always going to be the biographer.

**Career frames are culture packs.** The condottiere season negotiates the contract itself (profit missions, the employer as adversary-in-waiting); the Roman new man plays for the triumph, which means playing the *Senate's* belief DB as much as the enemy's; the samurai retainer's standing ledger is honor with a lord who may himself fall; the Victorian officer chases mentions-in-dispatches while the telegraph slowly murders the Camp station's comfortable distance. Same engine, same season clock — the career frame, the station price list, and the doctrine files are what a "faction" now *is*.

---

## 4 — What this does to scope (the quiet de-risk)

The commission frame shrinks the MVP again, and honestly. A playable game now requires **one army, one mission, one season** — the world beyond the commission can launch as a procedural patron and an off-screen war state rather than a full grand-strategy simulation, because the player never needed to own the world, only to be used by it. The build order from Addendum F survives intact with its target renamed: the CLI milestone is no longer "the campaign substrate" in the abstract, it is *a complete season, muster to interlude, in text*. The grand campaign — player as dynasty or state, the original Directions A and B at full scale — remains exactly what it was, now positioned as the mode you build *after* the season loop is fun, on the same substrate, with the career general available inside it as the most human way to play it.

---

## 5 — Risks

Season repetition is the Pirates! disease, and its cures are the ones Pirates! found plus one it lacked: mission taxonomy variety, the aging clock forcing escalation, a drifting war-state so the same mission is never the same world — and our addition, the receipts system itself, because a dealt army of Wexcombe levies is a genuinely different game from a dealt army of serjeants, all the way down to the lattice. The Front Rank station must never curdle into an action game: one lever, mostly witnessed, the cost paid in information — if a playtest reports it as "the fun fighting mode," the station is mispriced and the Caesar trade has been broken. Player death wants a policy: the default is the historical one (capture and ransom for the armored and important — which the player is), with death rare but real and culturally weighted, and an ironman toggle for those who want the *promachoi* bargain at full price. And the memoir is now load-bearing narrative software: if the chronicle generator writes mush, the career ends in mush, so it graduates from punchlist item to core feature with its own quality bar.

**Verdict:** adopted in full — the stations as the embodiment of Devereaux's typology with culture as the price list, the commission season as the chapter, the career as the game. **Punchlist:** station tuples (information set, lever set, anchor radius, lottery, latency, cultural price) added to the culture-file schema; the commission generator (patron, hand, mission, strings); the winter-interlude court scenes; capture-and-ransom flow for the player's own body; aging and scars on the protagonist's class-C sheet; the memoir compiler promoted to core; MVP redefined as one full season in the CLI.
