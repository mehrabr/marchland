# MARCHLAND — Addendum V: The Neighbors
### Market research, final pass — the closest games, the closest media, and whether the aesthetic already exists anywhere

**Extends:** N (personas) and U (the render direction). **Brief:** three questions. Does our system already exist as a game, even partially? What media — games, film, anything — sit closest to the whole vision? And does the Table-and-Window aesthetic exist in any art medium? The answers: every organ exists somewhere, no organism; the film kin are specific and old; and the aesthetic has existed three times as a *mass medium*, which is better news than if we'd invented it.

---

## 1 — Does the system exist? The organ map

**The command model — closest single ancestor: Command Ops.** Panther Games' operational series is the genre's serious attempt at our C2 layer: no hexes, no turns, no click-fests, "command like Commanders do" — AI subordinate commanders who plan and execute, with order delays that depend on officer quality, distance from HQ, and whether the recipient is busy. That is Addendum K's chain, shipped, for WWII at battalion grain. What it lacks: everything below (a conventional combat model underneath), everything above (no campaign body), and everything epistemic (its map is true). **General Staff: Black Powder** computes courier travel to the minute — 33 minutes for an order to pass from Ney through a subordinate HQ to a brigade — and then supplies this project's favorite cautionary datum: player demand made the developer ship a *"Game (no order delays)"* toggle alongside "Simulation." The market pull toward piloting, documented in a changelog. **Scourge of War's** HITS mode and its courier community we already mined (H); players of *other* games petition for it by name. And at the hobby's floor, an itch.io project literally titled **Kriegsspiel** runs the purist core — couriers only, fog not shared between general and troops, self-described as ugly and unstable — proof the demand reaches down to one-person projects. A separate terminal game on itch (sharing the name *Burden of Command*) plays the endurance register: dispatches from a distant HQ you decide whether to obey, a game that "does not ask you to win battles. It asks you to endure."

**The other organs, each alive somewhere:** resolver-realism RTT (Strategos: tabletop-derived morale shocks, formation disorder, C2 — but a ruleset, not a mechanistic substrate); operational logistics with a human staff layer (Decisive Campaigns: Barbarossa, Shadow Empire); supply-line campaign maps (Hegemony); watch-don't-pilot battles (Dominions, six iterations deep; Kessen, on a PS2); the judgment-of-men loop (Football Manager, Out of the Park); the dealt-hand mercenary career (Battle Brothers); the belief-map-by-radio (Radio Commander); the season-narrative with arguing advisors (King of Dragon Pass).

**The verdict, stated for the pitch deck:** every subsystem in this design has shipped, profitably, at least once — which de-risks each organ — and *no game combines more than two of them*, which is the opportunity. What exists nowhere at all, in any combination: the statless receipts substrate, the validated historical battery, ensemble adjudication, the reflexive chronicle, and the siege→march→battle chain. The nearest single neighbor is Command Ops wearing Scourge of War's saddle — and that is still only the battle-day command slice of a design whose center of gravity is the campaign, the career, and the lie-prone record.

## 2 — The closest media beyond games

**Legend of the Galactic Heroes** remains the founding citation: war as dispatches, councils, and operational geometry, where the battles are consequences and the drama is the waiting. **Kurosawa owns the seat.** *Kagemusha* is the station system as tragedy — the double who must sit motionless on the hill because the seat itself is the weapon, and the ending, an entire battle witnessed from the one place its owner cannot leave, is the Knot's covenant filmed forty years early. *Ran* is the Window: color-coded masses moving across landscape like paint deciding to march, battles watched from heights in compositions that are screen-paintings before they are shots. **Waterloo (1970)** is the photoreal Window's high-water mark — fifteen thousand real extras and Wellington's hill — and the budget lesson attached. **Barry Lyndon** is the aesthetic thesis in cinematography: every frame composed as the period's own painting, which is Addendum I said with candlelight. **Shōgun (2024)** is battle-by-consent as prestige television — a war won by council, maneuver, and one battle *threatened into never happening*, proof a mass audience will sit rapt through exactly the campaign our design centers. In prose: *War and Peace* under the battle layer, *The Face of Battle* under the resolver, *The Killer Angels* for courier drama — and the career frame's native genre was always the **military memoir**; Grant wrote the Page first.

## 3 — Does the aesthetic exist in art? Three times, as mass media

**Cinema has shipped the moving painting.** *Loving Vincent* is a feature film painted frame by frame in oils — the "tapestry that moves" exists, was profitable, and was nominated for an Academy Award. *The Mill and the Cross* goes further and films *inside* Bruegel's "Procession to Calvary" — the camera living within a period painting's space and logic, which is the Dive as an art-house feature. Ghost of Tsushima's Kurosawa mode proved players treat an era-styled render as a celebrated feature rather than a budget compromise.

**The Window was a Victorian industry.** The nineteenth-century **panorama and cyclorama** — Racławice, the Gettysburg Cyclorama, Mesdag — sold admission tickets to stand *inside* a 360° battle painting, complete with faux terrain merging canvas into floor. Battle painting as immersive spatial mass medium: our Window has a business-model ancestor, not just an aesthetic one.

**The handscroll already moves.** The Japanese *emakimono* — the Heiji Monogatari battle scrolls above all — are period battle art the viewer scrolls *through time*: continuous narrative, the same figures advancing across the length, the burning gate unrolling at reading speed. Sequential, durational period art of mass combat existed seven centuries before film; the Sengoku Window's art bible has a native motion grammar waiting in it. (The Bayeux is the same medium lying sideways.)

**And the Table exists in a museum.** William Siborne's Large Model of Waterloo (1838): a vast miniature of the battle's crisis moment, built from field survey and *hundreds of surviving officers' written recollections* — and famously contested, because the recollections disagreed; Wellington's circle disputed the Prussians' modeled positions, and Siborne revised the model under pressure. A diorama assembled from a belief database, whose finish encoded whose memory prevailed, controversial for exactly the reasons our Table is designed to dramatize. Uncertainty-as-finish has a nineteenth-century ancestor with a curation scandal.

## 4 — The positioning line

For the deck, the comp sentence the research supports: **Command Ops' chain of command, Dominions' covenant, Football Manager's judgment of men, and Kagemusha's seat — on the first engine that can prove its battles against the historical record, rendered the way each era painted its own wars.** Every clause has a shipped existence proof; the conjunction has none. That's the product.

**Verdict:** the system does not exist; its parts all do, which is the best possible answer — each organ de-risked by a predecessor, the organism unclaimed. The aesthetic is not an invention but a *revival* of three proven media (the painted film, the battle panorama, the war scroll) plus one museum object that anticipated the Table's epistemics by two centuries. **Punchlist:** the comp deck (organ map + media kin) into the pitch materials; Siborne, the Heiji scrolls, the cycloramas, and *The Mill and the Cross* into the art bible's reference shelf; General Staff's no-delay capitulation into the design bible's risk register as the named failure mode the covenant exists to refuse.
