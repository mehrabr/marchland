# MARCHLAND — Addendum H: The Three Rooms
### Interface and presentation — whether Total War's spectacle can live here, what Kessen and Nobunaga's Ambition actually teach, and the shape of the answer

**Extends:** Addenda A–G, especially G's closing risk: the simulation is now the easy part; the interface that makes preparation and interpretation feel like play is where the design lives or dies. **Brief:** research the presentation question properly. Can a Total War-grade visual spectacle coexist with this engine? Is the Kessen register or the Nobunaga's Ambition register a better fit? Or something else?

---

## 1 — Decompose before answering

"Interface" is three jobs wearing one trenchcoat, and Total War's real sin was fusing them, not performing any one of them badly.

The **command surface** is what your hands touch — where orders originate. The **witness surface** is what your eyes see live — where the battle is experienced. The **record surface** is what you study afterward — where understanding happens. Total War fuses all three into a single omniscient cursor-camera: the thing you watch with is the thing you command with is the thing you analyze with, simultaneously, from anywhere, instantly. Every Devereaux critique of the genre's command model is downstream of that fusion. The spectacle was never the problem. **The cursor was the problem — the camera is innocent.**

Which reframes the brief's question. "Can Total War spectacle work?" becomes: *in which room can it live without re-fusing the layers?* And the precedent research below says: in one of the three rooms, completely; in another, partially and diegetically; in the command room, never.

---

## 2 — What the precedents actually teach

### Kessen (2000) — the witness surface, proven on a PS2

Kessen's producer framed the project as the desire to make *a movie that could be controlled* — and the mechanics follow: the player issues orders from a field map, and the game cuts to cinematics showing the order executing; reviewers noted the field-map phase was dull but "it pays off when you can see the battles up close," with general duels and special maneuvers as the cinematic punctuation. Three lessons transfer directly. First, **watch-as-reward works**: a 2000-era console title built an entire battle experience on giving sparse orders and watching their consequences rendered with drama, and it shipped at a platform launch. Second, **the camera can be directed rather than free** — Kessen's cuts to where the action is were a feature, not a budget compromise. Third, the cautionary lesson: reviewers found the battle animations turned redundant after a few missions, because Kessen's cinematics were *canned* — a fixed library of clash animations decorating a stat resolution. Our engine inverts that economics: the variety Kessen's animators couldn't author is exactly what a mechanistic simulation generates for free. The tear at the third file from the ford is a different event every battle because it *is* a different event.

And one historical warning: Kessen III (2005) retreated to orders that "respond immediately," promoting "more fluid, intense warfare" — the market pulled Koei back toward piloting within two sequels. The pull is real and permanent. Our moat is structural: there is no stat layer underneath for a piloting interface to control. The design conviction is enforced by the architecture, which is the only enforcement that survives a publisher meeting.

### Nobunaga's Ambition — the desk, half-built for forty years

The series is map-first: the campaign is the game, battles resolve on the strategic map across multiple simultaneous fronts, with an optional close view. Two findings matter. The series already gestures at our epistemics — field troop counts display as *estimates* ("500, 1000, 1500") unless you've previously spied on the source castle — a fog-of-knowledge mechanic sitting quietly in a mainstream Koei title. And the series demonstrates the pathology our design exists to prevent: players report that manually fighting battles "really break[s] this game difficulty wise" versus letting the map resolve them — the **sim-versus-manual exploit**, where player piloting beats the resolver and the honest player must choose between fun and fairness. MARCHLAND cannot have this exploit because there is nothing to switch between: one resolver at every zoom, no manual override, the tiers sharing variables rather than offering escape hatches. Meanwhile the series' officer "Traits" and "Tactics" — passive auras and activated abilities — are the stat-essence trap rendered as UI, and a reviewer's complaint that "the physicality of conflict feels removed" names the cost of living entirely at the desk. The desk is necessary. It is not sufficient.

### Scourge of War's courier mode — proof the audience exists

The most important research finding is a community, not a feature. Scourge of War ships "Headquarters in the Saddle": camera locked to a 20-meter radius of your commander's horse, all knowledge arriving by courier. A player describes it: you send a rider to Longstreet asking for a report; *an hour later the courier arrives with information thirty minutes old*; you order the attack on its basis; by the time the order arrives, the situation has changed and the attack fails — "That's the realism that I love." Players in that community defend the interface's opacity on principle: part of the immersion of courier play *is not knowing everything*. This is our dispatch game, shipped, with a paying audience asking for more of it — and their forum threads are a free usability study. Two concrete UI lessons from their complaints: orders-in-flight need visible state (a player asked for the order button to change color between *sent* and *received* — the interface must render the latency it imposes, or latency reads as a bug), and doctrine-vocabulary friction is tolerated but must be learnable (a player needing a second courier because the first order produced the wrong formation is the repertoire system working — if the interface teaches the vocabulary, and infuriating if it doesn't).

### Three more, briefly

Radio Commander (2019) runs an entire commercial game on the dispatch layer alone: a Vietnam HQ tent, a paper map, a radio — units report positions by voice and the player moves map tokens *by hand* based on what they claim, which may be wrong. The belief database as a physical act. Dominions has shipped six iterations of watch-only battles — pre-battle scripting, autonomous resolution, replay you study but cannot touch — sustained for decades by a two-person team, proving the record surface carries weight even with placeholder graphics. And King of Dragon Pass demonstrated that an opportunity feed — situations arriving as illustrated correspondence, advisors arguing, the player sponsoring — can be the *entire* interface of a beloved strategy game. Every room we need has been built somewhere, profitably, by someone smaller than a Total War team. What has never been built is the house.

## 3 — The recommendation: three rooms, one truth

Match the un-fused layers to the tier architecture from Addendum F. The player moves between rooms; the engine's single trace feeds all three; no room lies, but each room knows a different amount.

**The Desk** — the command and campaign surface, in the Nobunaga/King of Dragon Pass register. The map is the belief database *rendered as a period document*: hand-drawn cartography that gains survey detail where your riders have been and fades toward rumor-hatching where they haven't; enemy presence drawn as a smear with a source annotation, never as a figurine. The opportunity feed arrives as correspondence — your marshal's proposal, the merchant's report, the ransom offer — and sponsoring one is signing it. Muster rolls, granary ledgers, the burn clock as an actual hourglass on the desk. This room is the CLI client grown a skin, which keeps the build order from Addendum F intact: the Desk is playable in month four as text and in year two as parchment.

**The Saddle** — the witness surface, in the Kessen-by-way-of-HITS register, used only when you ride with the army on a day of battle. The live camera is your vantage: the hill you chose, the staff around you, sightlines and dust doing what sightlines and dust do. The five levers (reserve, rehearsed signal, banner, advance, retreat) sit on a sparse order palette drawn from *your doctrine's repertoire and nothing else*, and dispatched orders ride visibly — a rider icon crossing the field, the order's UI state shifting from *sent* to *received*, per the Scourge of War lesson. Kessen's cinematic cuts return here transformed into something diegetic: when a report arrives from the left wing, the game may *play* the report — a directed-camera vignette of what the rider saw, clearly time-stamped minutes ago. The cinematic is stale on purpose. You are watching a memory delivered at a gallop, and the difference between the vignette and the present is the game's whole epistemology made visible. Spectacle in this room is the **legible lattice**: formation body language replaces bars — the dressing of a line loosening, banners tilting as files compress, the shudder that runs through a wing before it goes, rendered at whatever fidelity the Field tier affords within your sightline. Total War-grade rendering of *the visible portion* is welcome and right. Total War's cursor is not in the building.

**The Archive** — the record surface, and the answer to the spectacle question. After the battle, the full trace unlocks: free camera, total truth, time scrubbing — every Total War cinematic pleasure, granted *here*, because omniscience over history is the historian's legitimate stance rather than the commander's impossible one. Click any body for its death certificate. Drag the ensemble slider to watch the hundred Hastings and see which of them you actually fought. Export the chronicle. This room is where the post-battle screen becomes, as Addendum G demanded, the best history lesson in games — and it is nearly free to build, because the deterministic trace (inputs + seed) *is* the replay file, and the renderer that draws the Saddle draws this too.

So: **can the Total War spectacle work? Yes — demoted from a command interface to a reward.** Live, you get the spectacle your hill can see plus the stale cinematic memories your riders carry. Afterward, you get all of it, godlike, with explanations attached. The fantasy on the box stops being *control* and becomes *witness, then understanding* — which is, not coincidentally, the seat the primary sources were written from.

## 4 — Sound as the honest channel

One addition the precedent games barely touch and our engine makes cheap: in a pre-modern battle, **sound is the only real-time information channel** — the roar of contact, its direction, its movement, the awful specific change when a wing's noise turns from fighting to fleeing. The Saddle should treat audio as instrumentation: spatialized, truthful, faster than any rider. A player learning to *hear* that the left has gone before the dispatch confirms it is a player living inside Total Generalship I instead of reading about it. This is also the kindest solution to the boredom risk — the soundscape keeps the idle minutes tense, because the information never stops arriving; only the *confirmed* information does.

---

## 5 — Aesthetic direction, briefly

The UI is a *document stack*, period-correct per culture and century: the 1066 campaign's chronicle reads differently from the 1879 campaign's telegraph-clipped dispatches, and the map sharpens from portolan-style sketch to surveyed contours as the centuries and your intelligence improve — the same epistemics, costumed by era. Battle art direction spends its budget on **readable mass behavior** rather than gore particles: the things the engine actually computes (coverage, fatigue, cohesion, the tear) must be the things an eye can see at two hundred meters, which is a stylization problem, not a realism problem. Kessen's named-general duels return as **vignette events drawn from the real trace** — when the death certificate says the Earl's banner went down at the ford, the Saddle may cut to it, because it happened, in this seed, to that man.

---

## 6 — Feasibility and risks

Cost runs *below* a Total War-class production, not above it: watch-only and directed-camera modes delete the genre's most expensive UX surfaces (unit selection, pathing feedback, control responsiveness across a thousand edge cases); Kessen staged its drama on hardware from 2000; Dominions sustains the record surface with a team of two; the Archive's replay infrastructure is the deterministic trace we already require for other reasons. The risks are the ones the precedents flag. Kessen's redundancy problem — answered by simulation-generated variety, but only if the renderer can *show* that variety, so the legible-lattice art direction is load-bearing, not decorative. The Saddle's idle minutes — answered by the sound channel, the dispatch rhythm, and time compression between decision points, and this needs playtesting from the first CLI build onward, because if anticipation doesn't carry the experience in text it won't carry it in parchment. And the permanent market pull toward piloting that bent Kessen III — answered structurally: the engine has no second resolver to expose, no stat layer to click. The interface can't betray the design because the design left it nothing to betray it with.

**Verdict:** not Total War's room, not Kessen's alone, not Nobunaga's alone — the three rooms, one truth, with TW's spectacle alive and well in the Archive, Kessen reborn as the diegetic stale vignette, and Nobunaga's desk finally given the epistemics it has been gesturing at since the Famicom. **Punchlist:** order-in-flight UI states; the stale-vignette system tied to the trace; spatialized truth-audio; the ensemble slider; the per-era document skin; legible-lattice art bible as a first-class deliverable alongside the engine.
