# MARCHLAND — Addendum G: The Player's Seat
### One campaign, walked twice — where the experience diverges from Total War, which critique each divergence answers, and what the player actually gets to do

**Extends:** Addenda A–F. **Device:** the same military situation played in both games. It is late September. An invader has crossed the march with the better field army. You are the defender: fewer professionals, home ground, winter coming. We walk the beats in order; at each one, what Total War offers, what MARCHLAND offers instead, the Devereaux critique sitting under the divergence, and the lever the player gains.

---

## Beat 1 — Knowing where they are

**Total War:** you end your turn, the camera pulls back, and the enemy stack is a figurine on the map wearing its army size like a name tag. Hover for composition. The world is a chessboard with weather effects.

**MARCHLAND:** there is no enemy figurine because there is no enemy *position* — there is your belief database, rendered. The invader is a shaded smear across three road-strands, annotated: *vanguard seen at Harnford, four days stale, merchant-grade source. Foraging parties reported in the Wexcombe hundreds, two days, reliable.* The smear sharpens where your riders have been and rots where they haven't. Sometimes the smear is simply wrong, and the game never apologizes, because the chronicle is the only world you are ever shown.

**The critique answered:** Total Generalship I — the omniscient flying general who sees everything and knows it instantly is the genre's founding impossibility. **The lever gained:** intelligence becomes play. You spend riders, bribe carters, post watchers on hills; you choose between a tight cloud that sees little and a wide one that sees much and can be defeated in detail. In Total War, scouting is a chore done by a cheap unit. Here it is half the game, because acting on a four-day-old smear *is* the job description of every commander before the telegraph.

---

## Beat 2 — Moving an army that eats

**Total War:** the stack has movement points and spends them in any direction like a chess queen with stamina. Attrition is a corner-case debuff. The army is the same object in January and July.

**MARCHLAND:** the army is a probability cloud on a road graph, and the graph has a *carrying capacity* written by season and population. Concentrate the cloud into a fist and the burn clock lights: a fist this size standing in these hundreds eats them bare in nine days. Disperse and you can live indefinitely — but a dispersed army is an army that cannot fight today, only promise to fight in three. The autumn matters: the harvest is in, the granaries are full, which is precisely why the invader came *now* and why your Fabian option has a deadline — in November his cloud must either disperse into winter quarters or starve, and you both know the date.

**The critique answered:** the entire Logistics series — armies confined to the spiderweb of roads, decided by food more often than by swords, dispersing in winter not as a penalty modifier but as a law of nature. **The lever gained:** supply as a weapon. You strip the granaries on his axis of advance and fill the ones behind your chosen ground. You don't fight his army; you starve his *concentration*, forcing him to choose between dispersing (and being raided) and fighting on the field you fattened for the occasion. Total War has no verb for any of this. Here it is the main verb.

---

## Beat 3 — The battle that has to be agreed to

**Total War:** click the enemy stack. Battle occurs. The defender's consent is a loading screen.

**MARCHLAND:** battle is by consent or by trap, never by click. Engagements begin as *contexts* — two clouds' edges interpenetrating around a ford, a bridge, a line of ridges — and escalate only if someone accepts the terms or gets caught violating them. The invader offers battle on the open ground at Harnford; you decline, and your refusal costs you the chronicle's opinion and a sliver of the belief channel (men talk when their lord won't fight), but it costs him four more days of the burn clock. He tries to *make* it unrefusable: pins your forage parties, threatens the one bridge your cloud's halves share. Now the opportunity feed — the AI proposing, you sponsoring — surfaces the real choices: *Sir Aldric proposes a night withdrawal over the bridge, abandoning the southern granaries. The Earl's marshal argues the ford at Wexcombe can be held by 400 against any number for a day.* You sponsor one. The campaign has become a negotiation conducted in marches.

**The critique answered:** Devereaux's point that pitched battles were rare and consensual — most campaigns were maneuver, devastation, and siege, and a general who could always force battle is a fantasy that erases Fabius, erases the chevauchée, erases most of military history. **The lever gained:** *whether* — the single most consequential decision a pre-modern commander owned, restored from a loading screen to the center of play.

---

## Beat 4 — The day before, which is the real battle

**Total War:** a deployment phase: drag units inside a zone, then the whistle blows and the actual game — piloting — begins.

**MARCHLAND:** the day before is where the battle is mostly won or lost, and the interface treats it that way. You choose the ground from what the campaign gave you (the ridge with the marsh on its left exists because you maneuvered for three weeks to stand on it). You write the plan in your doctrine's vocabulary — and only in your doctrine's vocabulary: per-wing instructions, the conditions under which the reserve commits, what the signal for general advance will be, who holds the bridge if the day goes wrong. You set the things that will execute when you can no longer intervene: pursuit discipline (do we chase or hold), quarter policy (ransoms or no prisoners — a moral ledger the campaign will price), the baggage's position (your supply is *on this field*, and they know it). And you place your own body, once: the strongest appraisal anchor in the engine, near the wing you think will crack, accepting the arrow lottery that placement buys.

**The critique answered:** Total Generalship I's positive claim — the historical general's craft was ground, deployment, plan, reserves, presence, almost all of it spent *before* contact. **The lever gained:** the entire pre-battle suite stops being a thirty-second formality and becomes the game's chess. Total War players already half-know this — ask any multiplayer veteran where matches are won — but the engine then hands them a hundred mid-fight interventions that erase the deployment's meaning. We don't.

---

## Beat 5 — The fight, watched from a horse

**Total War:** time slows or pauses at will; the camera teleports; every unit obeys instantly and exactly; morale is a bar you read like a fuel gauge; abilities fire on cooldowns. You are not a general. You are twenty regimental commanders possessing one nervous system.

**MARCHLAND:** you see what your position sees, plus what galloping messengers bring — minutes stale, occasionally wrong, sometimes heroic fiction. Orders go out at horse speed *to banners, not to men*, and they arrive as requests made to people: a unit receiving an order outside its repertoire does not execute it badly, it does not execute it at all, because no one drilled it and the order is noise. A unit receiving a culturally illegible order — *stand and die so the levies can escape* — appraises it like the humans they are. Your levers during the fight number perhaps five: commit the reserve (the one decision Devereaux says battle-day generalship mostly was), signal a repertoire change the doctrine prepared (the feigned withdrawal you rehearsed in August — and rehearsal is a campaign verb with a receipt), ride your banner to a wavering wing, sound the general advance, or sound the retreat while a retreat is still a maneuver and not a massacre. Between those decisions you *watch* — and the watching is the spectacle, because the lattice is legible: you can see fatigue stagger a line, see the tear open where the fyrd chased the feint, see it repaired by file-closers or not repaired, and know, the way the sources always describe knowing, that the day has turned before any number tells you.

**The critique answered:** Total Generalship II in full — command latency, the drill-defined repertoire, the cultural limits on what can be ordered. **The lever gained:** fewer levers, each catastrophic. This is the trade the whole design makes, and it should be stated plainly: we remove the power fantasy of piloting and replace it with the older, stranger fantasy of *responsibility* — having set a thing in motion that no longer entirely obeys you.

---

## Beat 6 — How it ends

**Total War:** units fight until a morale bar empties, rout, often rally, and casualties accumulate steadily through the melee like a resource exchange. Annihilation is normal. The Bannerlord-class absurdity — battles where 96% of participants die — is the genre's resting state.

**MARCHLAND:** the lattice holds, and holds, and then *percolates* — coverage loses its giant component and the formation stops being a formation in under a minute, the way Adrianople and Hastings actually ended. Casualties to that point have been startlingly light, because they were never the mechanism; the mechanism was always the structure. Then the second battle begins, the one fought against backs: the pursuit, where history's casualties actually live, governed by the discipline you set yesterday and the quarter customs both armies carry. Men in harness are *taken* — the armor that protected them now prices them — and ransoms flow into the opportunity feed as revenue with names attached. Or you ordered no quarter, and the campaign's belief and allegiance systems begin pricing *that*. And sometimes you order the golden bridge: let them run unbutchered, because a broken army that escapes disperses along the road graph and re-coalesces in ten days as a problem, but a cornered one fights like the appraisal engine says cornered men fight.

**The critique answered:** Total Generalship IIIc — morale as two real channels instead of a bar, casualties concentrated after cohesion fails, the peasant-mob-that-holds absurdity made impossible because nothing in the engine *can* hold through what no humans held through. **The lever gained:** the pursuit decision, the quarter decision, the golden bridge — three verbs Total War cannot represent because its battles end where these begin.

---

## Beat 7 — The morning after, and the report you can't trust

**Total War:** a results screen with exact kill counts, then the wounded vanish, the stack replenishes by percentage, and the world forgets.

**MARCHLAND:** you get a chronicle, not a scoreboard — and the chronicle is a *document with a source*, written from what your side could see, subject to the same epistemics as everything else. It says the Earl's serjeants broke the levy at the ford; it does not know that two hundred of the levy re-formed at Wexcombe, because dispersal is not death (the Bull Run rule: a broken army whose belief held re-coalesces on the road graph). The ensemble's honesty gates the language: the chronicle writes "a near-run thing" only when the seed split earns it, so the player's sense of fortune is calibrated against the engine's actual counterfactuals — your court historian never flatters you. Meanwhile the battle writes upward into everything: carts of wounded slow the host and eat the stockpile; a village that lost its men is a changed node in the allegiance map; survivors carry new priors (the army that held learns it can hold); ransoms pay the chest that holds the bond edges together. The campaign and the battle were never two games. They were one ledger, read at two speeds.

**The critique answered:** the casualty-structure and aftermath arguments again, plus the deeper one underneath the whole ACOUP corpus — that war is a social activity conducted by institutions and communities, not an exchange of hit points between stacks. **The lever gained:** aftermath as strategy. Prisoner negotiations, wound care (a campaign investment with mortality curves), where the broken enemy will re-form and whether to be there first, what story your own dispatches will tell the capital.

## The lever inventory

Set side by side, the interactivity trade looks like this:

| phase | Total War's verbs | MARCHLAND's verbs |
|---|---|---|
| intelligence | hover the enemy figurine | spend riders; rate sources; widen or tighten the cloud; plant false dispatches; build the dummy camp |
| movement & supply | spend movement points | choose strands; set dispersion width; light or bank the burn clock; strip and stock granaries; time the muster against the 40-day clocks |
| forcing battle | click the stack | offer, refuse, bait, pin; trade chronicle-opinion for clock-time; sponsor opportunities from the feed |
| the day before | drag units in a zone | ground, order of battle, per-wing doctrine, reserve conditions, signals, pursuit & quarter policy, baggage, your own body |
| during | pause; pilot 20 units; fire abilities | commit the reserve; signal rehearsed repertoire changes; move the banner; sound advance or retreat; *watch and understand* |
| ending | fight to the bar's end | pursue or hold; quarter or not; the golden bridge |
| after | replenish percentage | ransom negotiations; wounded logistics; intercept the re-forming enemy; shape the dispatch home |

Count them. The piloting game lost perhaps thirty verbs and gained perhaps thirty different ones — the interactivity budget is conserved, but it moved *up the chain of command and out across the calendar*, which is exactly where Devereaux locates the historical general's actual decisions. The design claim is not "less gameplay, more realism." It is that the realism *relocates* the gameplay to where the interesting decisions always were.

---

## What Total War gets right, and we must not lose

Honesty about the competitor. Total War delivers three pleasures this design endangers and must consciously replace. **Spectacle:** the cavalry wedge hitting a flank is one of the great sights in games; our equivalent must be the *legible lattice* — the tear, the repair, the stagger — rendered so a player can read a battle's health the way the engine does, plus the Field-tier flagship moments where the camera earns its keep. **Constant agency:** TW never leaves your hands idle; we deliberately do, so the idle time must be *tense* rather than empty — the dispatch rhythm is the dramatic engine (the Legend of the Galactic Heroes register from the original seed: the report is coming, and you already gave the only order that matters). **Legibility of cause:** TW's stats are bad history but honest UI — you always know *why* you lost. Our answer has to be better, not vaguer: the death certificates, the chronicle's receipts, the counterfactual replay ("starve the serjeants five days and the fatigue curves cross") give the player a *truer* why than any tooltip. If the post-battle screen is the best history lesson in games, the sparse-agency trade pays for itself. If it's a wall of mush, the whole design fails on contact with its first Tuesday-night player.

That is the project's real risk surface, named once more so nobody forgets it while admiring the engine: the simulation is the easy part now. The interface that makes preparation, anticipation, and interpretation *feel like playing* — that is where the remaining design genius has to go.
